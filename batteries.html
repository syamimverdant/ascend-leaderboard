1. Function of Solar PV Batteries

What Does a Solar Battery Do?

A solar battery stores excess energy generated by solar panels during the day so it can be used later — at night, during peak hours, or during power outages.

Why Do Customers Need a Battery?

  • Energy Independence — Reduce reliance on TNB grid. Use your own solar energy 24/7, not just during sunlight hours.
  • Backup Power — Keep critical appliances running during blackouts (lights, fridge, WiFi, fans).
  • Maximise Solar Savings — Without a battery, excess solar energy goes back to the grid at a lower rate. With a battery, you store it and use it yourself at full value.
  • Peak Shaving — Use stored solar energy during expensive peak tariff hours instead of buying from the grid.
  • Future-Proofing — As energy costs rise and NEM policies evolve, having stored energy gives homeowners more control.
💡 Key Selling Point: A solar system without a battery is like a water tank without a tap at night. The battery lets customers use their solar investment around the clock — not just when the sun is shining.

How Does It Work in a Solar System?

The flow is simple:

  1. Daytime: Solar panels generate electricity → powers the home → excess charges the battery
  2. Battery Full: Once the battery is full, remaining excess goes to the grid (NEM export)
  3. Evening/Night: Solar panels stop generating → battery discharges to power the home
  4. Battery Empty: If the battery runs out, the home draws from the grid as normal
  5. Blackout: The battery provides backup power to essential loads (if EPS is configured)
💡 Note for SCs: The hybrid inverter is the "brain" that manages this entire flow automatically. The customer doesn't need to do anything — it switches between solar, battery, and grid seamlessly.

💡 For SCs: If a customer asks "what type of battery?" — the answer is Lithium Iron Phosphate (LFP). It's the safest, longest-lasting, and most widely used for residential solar storage globally.

By Voltage Classification

This is the main focus of this training. Solar batteries are classified into two voltage categories:

ClassificationVoltage RangeDescription
High Voltage (HV) Our Standard 100V – 500V+ Batteries connected in series to achieve high voltage. Works with hybrid inverters designed for HV input. More efficient energy transfer.
Low Voltage (LV) Alternative 48V – 51.2V Batteries operating at lower voltage, typically connected in parallel. Requires higher current for same power output.

Why Does Voltage Matter?

The basic electrical formula explains everything:

Power (W) = Voltage (V) × Current (A)

To deliver the same power:
• High Voltage = lower current needed → more efficient, less heat, thinner cables
• Low Voltage = higher current needed → more energy loss, more heat, thicker cables

Example: To deliver 5,000W of power:

High Voltage (400V)Low Voltage (48V)
Current Required5000 ÷ 400 = 12.5A5000 ÷ 48 = 104A
Cable SizeThinner (lower current)Much thicker (8× more current)
Energy Loss (I²R)LowerHigher

Think of it like a water hose. HV batteries are like a high-pressure hose with a narrow nozzle — same amount of water, delivered faster and more efficiently. 4. Advantages & Disadvantages

High Voltage (HV) Batteries Our Standard

Advantages:

✅ Higher Efficiency — Less energy wasted as heat. More of the stored energy actually reaches the home's appliances.
✅ Faster Charge & Discharge — Can handle higher power output, meaning it responds faster when the home needs energy (e.g., turning on aircon, kettle).
✅ Cleaner Installation — Thinner cables mean a tidier installation with less visual clutter. Professional appearance.
✅ Better for Whole-Home Backup — Can power high-demand loads (aircon, water heater) more effectively during outages.
✅ Future-Proof — Industry trend is moving toward HV. Compatible with next-generation inverters and smart home systems.
✅ Longer Lifespan — Typically rated for more cycles, meaning longer years of service before replacement needed.
✅ Less Energy Loss Over Distance — If the battery is installed further from the inverter, HV loses less energy in transmission.

Disadvantages:

⚠️ Higher Upfront Cost — HV battery systems typically cost more than LV equivalents. However, the long-term efficiency gains and longer lifespan offset this over time.
⚠️ Requires Certified Installation — Higher voltage means stricter safety protocols. Must be installed by trained professionals (this is standard at Verdant).
⚠️ Inverter Pairing — HV batteries require compatible hybrid inverters. Not all inverters support HV input — but all modern premium inverters do.

Low Voltage (LV) Batteries Alternative

Advantages:

✅ Lower Entry Cost — More affordable upfront, making it accessible for budget-conscious customers.
✅ Wider Inverter Compatibility — Works with a broader range of inverters, including older or budget models.
✅ Easier Handling — Lower voltage means less safety risk during installation and maintenance.
✅ Simple Expansion — Adding more capacity is straightforward — connect additional units in parallel.

Disadvantages:

⚠️ Lower Efficiency — Higher current means more energy lost as heat in cables and connections.
⚠️ Thicker Cabling — Requires heavier gauge cables which can be harder to route and less aesthetically pleasing.
⚠️ Slower Response — May struggle to deliver high power quickly for demanding loads (aircon startup, etc.).
⚠️ Shorter Lifespan — Typically fewer rated cycles compared to HV equivalents.
⚠️ Limited Scalability for Large Systems — Parallel connections add complexity and can create imbalance issues at larger scales.
